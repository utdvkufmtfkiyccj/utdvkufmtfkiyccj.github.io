【本の内容】
This collection examines the relationships between the reproduction of people and the intergenerational transmission of memory considering historical traumas among the Igbo and Bamileke; the impact of AIDS in Botswana; parenting in Yorubaland; the evocation of age-sets in Samburu games; and Beng reminisciens of French colonialism. Together they demonstrate how by remembering the past in particular ways people anticipate the futures of their children.
The essays originated from two sessions of the American Anthropological Association organized by the editor of the journal
Africa
in order to highlight the intimate links between social and human reproduction and to place them in the cultural political and economic contexts of remembered histories. They explore how collective memories of historical events and reproductive crises frame reproductive behavior broadly conceived as encompassing fertility childrearing and a people"s demographic and social survival. They thus deal with basic anthropological issues regarding cultural distinctiveness and continuity.
Jennifer Cole"s foreword synthesizes the major issues relates them to studies of collective and social memory and introduces the ethnographic contributions. Jane Guyer"s Postscript completes the frame pointing the way toward future work on collective memory and reproduction in Africa. Together with
Grandparents and Grandchildren
edited by Wenzel Geissler Erdmute Alber and Susan Whyte (also available in the Special Issue Series of
Africa
) this volume demonstrates the vitality of a new wave of kinship studies in Africa.
